summ: <h3>Lesson 1; All habits are based on a four-step pattern, which consists of cue, craving, response, and reward.</h3><br><p>In 1776, Adam Smith laid the foundation of modern economics in his&nbsp;<em>magnum opus</em>,&nbsp;<em>The Wealth of Nations</em>.&nbsp;One of his most famous observations is that, in a free market system, all workers naturally maximize their own society&rsquo;s welfare, even if merely acting in their own best interest;</p><br><blockquote><br><p><em>&ldquo;&hellip;he is in this, as in many other cases, led by&nbsp;an&nbsp;invisible hand&nbsp;to promote an end which was no part of his intention.&rdquo;</em></p><br></blockquote><br><p>When it comes to habits, James suggests that the <strong>environment is the invisible hand that shapes human behavior.&nbsp;</strong>That&rsquo;s why a prompt is always the&nbsp;first step in performing any habit. It may not&nbsp;<em>always</em>&nbsp;be external, but, most of the time, it will be. Then, three more stages follow to complete the four-step pattern;</p><br><ol><br><li><strong>Cue</strong>. A piece of information that suggests there&rsquo;s a reward to be found, like the smell of a cookie or a dark room waiting to light up.</li><br><li><strong>Craving</strong>. The motivation to change something to get the reward, like tasting the delicious cookie or being able to see.</li><br><li><strong>Response</strong>. Whatever thought or action you need to take to get to the reward.</li><br><li><strong>Reward</strong>. The satisfying feeling you get from the change, along with the lesson of whether to do it again or not.</li><br></ol><br><h3>Lesson 2; To form habits, you must make them obvious, attractive, easy, and satisfying.</h3><br><p>From the four-step pattern he suggests, James then derives four laws of behavior change, which correspond to one part of the loop each. Here they are, along with some ideas for how you can use them to facilitate good behaviors and make bad ones harder;</p><br><ol><br><li><strong>Make it obvious</strong>. Don&rsquo;t hide your fruits in your fridge, put them on display front and center.</li><br><li><strong>Make it attractive</strong>. Start with the fruit you like the most, so you&rsquo;ll actually want to eat one when you see it.</li><br><li><strong>Make it easy</strong>. Don&rsquo;t create needless friction by focusing on fruits that&nbsp;are hard to peel. Bananas and apples are super easy to eat, for example.</li><br><li><strong>Make it satisfying</strong>. If you like the fruit you picked, you&rsquo;ll love eating it&nbsp;<em>and</em>&nbsp;feel healthier as a result!</li><br></ol><br><p>You can apply these to all kinds of good habits, like running, working on a&nbsp;side project, spending more time with family, and so on. Conversely, do the opposite for bad habits. Make them invisible, unattractive, difficult, and unsatisfying. For example, you could hide your cigarettes, add financial penalties, get rid of all lighters, and only allow yourself to smoke outside in the cold.</p><br><h3>Lesson 3; A habit tracker is a fun and easy way to ensure you stick to your new behaviors.</h3><br><p>With a framework like this, making and breaking habits becomes fun. You&rsquo;ll likely want to tackle multiple things sooner rather than later, but it&rsquo;s important to not take on too much at once. An easy way of keeping yourself accountable without becoming overwhelmed is to track your habits with&nbsp;a habit tracker.</p><br><p>The idea is simple;&nbsp;<strong>You keep a record of all the behaviors you want to establish or abandon and, at the end of each day, you mark which ones you succeeded with</strong>. This record can be a single piece of paper, a journal, a calendar, or a digital tool, like an app.</p><br><p>This strategy is based on what&rsquo;s sometimes called&nbsp;the Seinfeld productivity hack. Comedian Jerry Seinfeld apparently marked his calendar with a big &lsquo;X&rsquo; every day he came up with a joke. Soon, his goal was to not break the chain. It&rsquo;s a simple, but effective strategy to help you build good habits.</p><br><p>And since&nbsp;habits are the compound interest of self-improvement, that&rsquo;s a process we should all start today.</p><br>

<p>Japanese firms emphasized what came to be known as ‘lean production,’ relentlessly looking to remove waste of all kinds from the production process, down to redesigning workspaces, so workers didn’t have to waste time twisting and turning to reach their tools. The result was that Japanese factories were more efficient</p>

<p>Human behavior follows the Law of Least Effort. We will naturally gravitate toward the option that requires the least amount of work. Create an environment where doing the right thing is as easy as possible. Reduce the friction associated with good behaviors. When friction is low, habits are easy. Increase the friction associated with bad behaviors. When friction is high, habits are difficult. Prime your environment to make future actions easier.</p>

<p>People often think it’s weird to get hyped about reading one page or meditating for one minute or making one sales call. But the point is not to do one thing. The point is to master the habit of showing up. The truth is, a habit must be established before it can be improved. If you can’t learn the basic skill of showing up, then you have little hope of mastering the finer details. Instead of trying to engineer a perfect habit from the start, do the easy thing on a more consistent basis. You have to standardize before you can optimize.</p>

<p>Many habits occur at decisive moments—choices that are like a fork in the road—and either send you in the direction of a productive day or an unproductive one. The Two-Minute Rule states, “When you start a new habit, it should take less than two minutes to do.” The more you ritualize the beginning of a process, the more likely it becomes that you can slip into the state of deep focus that is required to do great things.</p>

<p>Here’s the problem: most people know that delaying gratification is the wise approach. They want the benefits of good habits: to be healthy, productive, at peace. But these outcomes are seldom top-of-mind at the decisive moment. Thankfully, it’s possible to train yourself to delay gratification—but you need to work with the grain of human nature, not against it. The best way to do this is to add a little bit of immediate pleasure to the habits that pay off in the long-run and a little bit of immediate pain to ones that don’t.</p>

<p>You don’t realize how valuable it is to just show up on your bad (or busy) days. Lost days hurt you more than successful days help you.
